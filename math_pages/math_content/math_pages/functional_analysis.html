Function Spaces

Given a field, say \( \mathbb{F} \) (usually either \( \mathbb{R} \) or \( \mathbb{C} \) ), a vector space over \( \mathbb{F} \), denoted \( V \), is a nonempty set that is closed under vector addition (+), and scalar multiplication \((\cdot)\), and satisfies the following axioms:
Associativity \( u + (v + w) = (u + v) + w \)
Commutitivity \( u + v = v + u \)
Additive Identity \( \exists \vec{0} \in V \) such that \( u + \vec{0} = u, \quad \forall u \in V \)
Additive Inverse \( \forall u \in V, \quad \exists (-u) \in V \) such that \( u + (-u) = \vec{0} \)
Multiplicative Identity (in \( \mathbb{F} \) ) \( \exists 1 \in \mathbb{F} \) such that \( 1u = u, \quad \forall u \in V \)
Distributive with respect to vector addition \( a(u + v) = au + av \quad \forall u, v \in V \) and \( \forall a \in \mathbb{F} \)
Distributive with respect to scalar addition \( (a + b)u = au + bu \quad \forall u \in V \) and \( \forall a, b \in \mathbb{F} \)
  The space \( V = \mathbb{R}^n \) is a vector space over the field \( \mathbb{R} \) with vector addition and scalar multiplication defined in the usual way for \( n \geq 1 \).
Note that by definition, \( v \in V \) is true if and only if \( v = \langle v_1, v_2, ..., v_n \rangle \) with \( v_i \in \mathbb{R} \) for \( i \in \{1, 2, ..., n\} \)
Hence, we can see that \( V \) is closed under vector addition and scalar multiplication very trivially: Let \( u, v \in V \) and \( a \in \mathbb{R} \). \begin{align*} u + v &= \langle u_1, u_2, ..., u_n \rangle + \langle v_1, v_2, ..., v_n \rangle \\ &= \langle u_1 + v_1, u_2 + v_2, ..., u_n + v_n \rangle, \end{align*} and \( u_i + v_i \in \mathbb{R} \) for all \( i \), hence \( u + v \in V \) and \( V \) is closed under vector addition. Similarly, we have, \begin{align*} au &= a \langle u_1, u_2, ..., u_n \rangle \\ &= \langle au_1, au_2, ..., au_n \rangle \in V \end{align*} and \( a u_i \in \mathbb{R} \) for all \( i \), hence \( au \in V \) and \( V \) is closed under scalar multiplication.
The remaining axioms are all pretty trivial to show since all are true due to the way vector addition and scalar mulitplication are defined. Let \( u, v, w \in V \) and \( a, b \in \mathbb{R} \) and we will prove all the axioms here:
Associativity \begin{align*} u + (v + w) &= \langle u_1, u_2, ..., u_n \rangle + (\langle v_1, v_2, ..., v_n \rangle + \langle w_1, w_2, ..., w_n \rangle) \\ &= \langle u_1, u_2, ..., u_n \rangle + \langle v_1 + w_1, v_2 + w_2, ..., v_n + w_n \rangle \\ &= \langle u_1 + (v_1 + w_1), u_2 + (v_2 + w_2), ..., u_n + (v_n + w_n) \rangle \\ &= \langle (u_1 + v_1) + w_1, (u_2 + v_2) + w_2, ..., (u_n + v_n) + w_n \rangle \\ &= \langle u_1 + v_1, u_2 + v_2, ..., u_n + v_n \rangle + \langle w_1, w_2, ..., w_n \rangle \\ &= (\langle u_1, u_2, ..., u_n \rangle + \langle v_1, v_2, ..., v_n \rangle) + \langle w_1, w_2, ..., w_n \rangle \\ &= (u + v) + w \end{align*} Commutitivity \begin{align*} u + v &= \langle u_1, u_2, ..., u_n \rangle + \langle v_1, v_2, ..., v_n \rangle \\ &= \langle u_1 + v_1, u_2 + v_2, ..., u_n + v_n \rangle \\ &= \langle v_1 + u_1, v_2 + u_2, ..., v_n + u_n \rangle \\ &= \langle v_1, v_2, ..., v_n \rangle + \langle u_1, u_2, ..., u_n \rangle \\ &= v + u \end{align*} Additive Identity
Define \( \vec{0} := \langle 0, 0, ..., 0 \rangle \) and note that \begin{align*} u + \vec{0} &= \langle u_1, u_2, ..., u_n \rangle + \langle 0, 0, ..., 0 \rangle \\ &= \langle u_1 + 0, u_2 + 0, ..., u_n + 0 \rangle \\ &= \langle u_1, u_2, ..., u_n \rangle \\ &= u \end{align*} Hence, \( \vec{0} \) is the additive identity in \( V \).
Additive Inverse
Given \( u \in V \) define \( -u := \langle -u_1, -u_2, ..., -u_3 \rangle \) and note that \begin{align*} u + (-u) &= \langle u_1, u_2, ..., u_n \rangle + \langle -u_1, -u_2, ..., -u_n \rangle \\ &= \langle u_1 + (-u_1), u_2 + (-u_2), ..., u_n + (-u_n) \rangle \\ &= \langle 0, 0, ..., 0 \rangle \\ &= \vec{0} \end{align*} Hence, \( -u \) is the additive inverse of \( u \).
Multiplicative Identity (in \(\mathbb{F}\))
Note that the multiplicative identity in \( \mathbb{R} \) is \(1\) since, \begin{align*} 1u &= 1\langle u_1, u_2, ..., u_n \rangle \\ &= \langle 1 \cdot u_1, 1 \cdot u_2, ..., 1 \cdot u_n \rangle \\ &= \langle u_1, u_2, ..., u_n \rangle \\ &= u \end{align*} Distributive with respect to vector addition \begin{align*} a(u + v) &= a(\langle u_1, u_2, ..., u_n \rangle + \langle v_1, v_2, ..., v_n \rangle) \\ &= a(\langle u_1 + v_1, u_2 + v_2, ..., u_n + v_n \rangle) \\ &= \langle a(u_1 + v_1), a(u_2 + v_2), ..., a(u_n + v_n) \rangle \\ &= \langle au_1 + av_1, au_2 + av_2, ..., au_n + av_n \rangle \\ &= a\langle u_1, u_2, ..., u_n \rangle + a\langle v_1, v_2, ..., v_n \rangle \\ &= au + av \end{align*} Distributive with respect to scalar multiplication \begin{align*} (a+b)u &= (a+b)\langle u_1, u_2, ..., u_n \rangle \\ &= \langle (a+b)u_1, (a+b)u_2, ..., (a+b)u_n \rangle \\ &= \langle au_1 + bu_1, au_2 + bu_2, ..., au_n + bu_n \rangle \\ &= a\langle u_1, u_2, ..., u_n \rangle + b\langle u_1, u_2, ..., u_n \rangle \\ &= au + bu \end{align*}
  The space \( V = L^p(\mathbb{R}) := \{ f : \int_{\mathbb{R}} |f|^p d\lambda < \infty \} \) is a vector space for \( 1 \leq p < \infty \)
Given a vector space \( V \) over some field \( \mathbb{F} \), a norm, say \( ||\cdot|| : V \rightarrow \mathbb{F} \), is a function with the following properties:
Tringle Inequality \( || v + u || \leq ||v|| + ||u|| \quad \forall u,v \in V \)
Absolute Homogeneity \( || \alpha u || = |\alpha| \cdot ||u|| \quad \forall u \in V \) and \( \forall \alpha \in \mathbb{F} \)
Positive Definiteness/Point Separating Given some \(u \in V\) then \( ||u|| = 0 \) if and only if \( u = 0 \)
A vector space on which a norm can be defined is called a normable vector space. There are examples of vector spaces that can not have norms, but the examples are quite technical and will be avoided for now. The pairing of a vector space \(V\), with a norm \(||\cdot||\) on \(V\), denoted by \( (V, ||\cdot||) \), is called a normed vector space.
  \( (\mathbb{R}, |\cdot| ) \) is a normed vector space.
  \( (\mathbb{R}^n, ||\cdot||_{p} ) \) is a normed vector space, where \( ||\cdot||_{p} := \left( \sum_{i=1}^n |v_i|^p\right)^{1/p} \) and \( 1 \leq p < \infty \).
  \( (\mathbb{R}^n, ||\cdot||_{\infty} ) \) is a normed vector space, where \( ||\cdot||_{\infty} := \max_{1 \leq i \leq n} |v_i| \).
  \( (L^p(\mathbb{R}), ||\cdot||_{L^p(\mathbb{R})} ) \) is a normed vector space, where \( ||f||_{L^p(\mathbb{R})} := \left( \int_{\mathbb{R}} |f|^p dx \right)^{1/p} \).
Let \( V \) be a vector space let two norms be defined on \( V \), say \( ||\cdot||_1 \) and \( ||\cdot||_2 \). We say that the two norms \( ||\cdot||_1 \) and \( ||\cdot||_2 \) are equivalent if there exist two real constants \( c > 0 \) and \( C \) such that \begin{align*} c||v||_1 \leq ||v||_2 \leq C||v||_1 \qquad \forall v \in V \end{align*}
All norms on a finite-dimensional vector space are equivalent
Let \( V \) be a normable finite-dimensional vector space. So then let \( \{x_i\}_{i=1}^n \) be a basis of \( V \) and note that for any \( x \in V \) we can rewrite this vector as follows: \( x = \sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_i x_i \) for some constants \( \alpha_i \in \mathbb{F} \). So now notice that we can define a norm as follows: \begin{align*} ||x||_1 := \sum_{i=1}^n |\alpha_i| \end{align*} Now let \( ||\cdot||_2 \) be an arbitrary norm on \( V \) and we notice the claim will be proven true if we can instead show that \( ||\cdot||_1 \) is equivalent to \( ||\cdot||_2 \). So now let \( x \in V \) such that \( ||x||_1 = 1 \) and notice that we have the following: \begin{align*} ||x||_2 &= \Big|\Big|\sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_i x_i \Big|\Big|_2 \\ &\leq \sum_{i=1}^n |\alpha_i| \cdot ||x_i||_2 \\ &\leq \sum_{i=1}^n |\alpha_i| \cdot \Big[\max_{1 \leq i \leq n} ||x_i||_2\Big] \\ &\leq \Big[\max_{1 \leq i \leq n} ||x_i||_2\Big] \sum_{i=1}^n |\alpha_i| \\ &= \Big[\max_{1 \leq i \leq n} ||x_i||_2\Big] ||x||_1, \end{align*} and by choosing \( c = 1 / \Big[\max_{1 \leq i \leq n} ||x_i||_2\Big] \) we get the following: \begin{align*} ||x||_2 \leq \frac{1}{c} ||x||_1 &\implies c||x||_2 \leq ||x||_1 \end{align*}
