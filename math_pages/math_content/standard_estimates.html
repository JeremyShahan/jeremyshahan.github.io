Grönwall's Inequality

There are several different forms of Grönwall's inequality. Here we mention three different types: the differential form, integral form, and discrete form.

Grönwall's Inequality (Differential Form)
Let \( I = [a, \infty) \) and let \( \beta \) and \( u \) be real-valued continuous functions defined on \( I \). If \( u \) is differentiable in the interior \( I^{\circ} \) of \( I \) and satisfies the differential inequality. \begin{align} u'(t) \leq \beta(t) u(t), \qquad \forall t \in I^{\circ}, \end{align} then \( u \) is bounded by the solution of the corresponding differential equation \( v'(t) = \beta(t)v(t) \). I.e. \begin{align} u(t) \leq u(a) \; \text{exp} \left(\int_a^t \beta(s) ds\right) \end{align}
Define \( v(t) \) so that it is the solution of the following ODE \begin{align} v'(s) & = \beta(s)v(s) \\ v(a) & = 1 \end{align} So then we have \begin{align} v'(s) = \beta(s)v(s) & \iff \int_a^t \frac{v'(s)}{v(s)} ds = \int_a^t \beta(s) ds, \\ & \iff \ln|v(t)| - \ln|v(a)| = \int_a^t \beta(s) ds, \\ & \iff \ln|v(t)| = \int_a^t \beta(s) ds, \\ & \iff |v(t)| = \text{exp} \left( \int_a^t \beta(s) ds \right), \\ & \iff v(t) = \text{exp} \left( \int_a^t \beta(s) ds \right), \\ \end{align} where the last line is true due to the fact that \( \text{exp} \left( \int_a^t \beta(s) ds > 0 \right) \) for all \( t \in I \) and \( v \) is continuous. This implies that either \( v < 0 \) or \( v > 0 \) for all \( t \in I \). Due to \( v(a) = 1 \) we have that \( v > 0 \) for all \( t \in I \).

So now consider the following, using the quotient rule \begin{align} \frac{d}{dt} \frac{u(t)}{v(t)} = \frac{u'(t)v(t) - u(t)v'(t)}{v^2(t)} = \frac{u'(t)v(t) - u(t)\beta(t)v(t)}{v^2(t)} = \frac{u'(t) - u(t)\beta(t)}{v(t)} \leq 0. \end{align} Hence, it follows that \begin{align} \frac{u(t)}{v(t)} \leq \frac{u(a)}{v(a)} & \iff \frac{u(t)}{v(t)} \leq u(a) \\ & \iff u(t) \leq u(a)v(t) \\ & \iff u(t) \leq u(a)\text{exp} \left( \int_a^t \beta(s) ds \right) \\ \end{align}
Grönwall's Inequality (Integral Form)
Let \( I = [a, \infty) \) and let \( \alpha \), \( \beta \), and \( u \) be real-valued functions defined on \( I \). Assume that \( \beta \) and \( u \) are continuous and that the negative part of \( \alpha \) is integrable on every closed and bounded subinterval of \( I \). If \( \beta \) is non-negative and if \( u \) satisfies the inequality \begin{align} u(t) \leq \alpha(t) + \int_a^t \beta(s) u(s) ds, \qquad \forall t \in I, \end{align} then \begin{align} u(t) \leq \alpha(t) + \int_a^t \alpha(s) \beta(s) \text{exp} \left( \int_s^t \beta(r) dr \right) ds, \qquad \forall t \in I. \end{align} If, in addition, the function \( \alpha \) is non-decreasing, then \begin{align} u(t) \leq \text{exp} \left( \int_a^t \beta(s) ds \right), \qquad \forall t \in I. \end{align}
Define \( v(t) \) as follows: \begin{align} v(t) := \text{exp} \left( - \int_a^t \beta(r) dr \right) \int_a^t \beta(r) u(r) dr, \forall s \in I. \end{align} So then it follows that \begin{align} v'(s) = \left( u(s) - \int_a^s \beta(r) u(r) dr \right) \beta(s) \text{exp}\left( - \int_a^s \beta(r) dr \right), \qquad \forall s \in I. \end{align} And now integrating \( v'(s) \) from \( a \) to \( t \) yields the following: \begin{align} & \int_a^t v'(s) ds = \int_a^t \left( u(s) - \int_a^s \beta(r) u(r) dr \right) \beta(s) \text{exp}\left( - \int_a^s \beta(r) dr \right) ds \\ \iff & v(t) = \int_a^t \left( u(s) - \int_a^s \beta(r) u(r) dr \right) \beta(s) \text{exp}\left( - \int_a^s \beta(r) dr \right) ds \\ \end{align}
